How will we test our product?
The basis of our product testing will be built upon low fidelity mockups of artefacts and then eventually they will be carried over to real, built prototypes using high fidelity mockups. This is so the team can focus on the basic design and flow rather than the intricate details which are unnecessary at this point in time for basic testing.

For our final prototypes our general test base will be with friends and family members However, due to the restrictions and COVID-19 Restrictions we are unable to do so with the physical probe prototype. This has, in turn meant that the lead designer, Nikhil Govula is the only one able to test the prototype as he has built it and has only one model, unfortunately he could not get other people to test it.

Eventually, the future plan for our testing would be to: first of all, have multiple working prototypes. We would likely be hiring a market research agency that will go out and find or look for consumers/product testers based on our target demographic or our specific set of requirements for each candidate. We are looking for botanists and newcomers to gardening to test our app and probe. Another way is to create an event at a public place and try to incentivise people to attend and test our product. This event could be posted onto social media and/or spread through word of mouth across a range of people fit for testing. We would want to test our product 3 different times with around 20 different people each time, preferably the ones with a lot of interest in gardening and plants in general. 60 in total. This is to develop a variation of results on people’s experiences with using the app.

The first testing session will be based around our initial working prototype, The following testing session will be for any other major revisions and additions made to the prototype. The third and final testing would be for the final product where we make our last changes according to the testers’ feedback wherever ever necessary. This same method of testing will also be done for the app as well. Like the product testing, we would have to find 60 different people, preferably the ones that have an interest in technology and new and upcoming inventions and/or technologies.

In the testing phase, we will be utilizing 2 different types of user testing methods, UAT and BVT. The UAT (user acceptance test), also known as beta testing, will generally apply for the software side of the testing where the users test the app. In this method of testing, users are trying to find any possible way to break it, this is done before product launch whilst still in the prototype testing phase. This could also apply to the hardware aspect of the product but will be done on a minimal scale. This is because we don’t want our product to completely break or get destroyed as we are just looking to gain information on the weaknesses of the product, and hopefully a strategy on how to fix or compensate for the weaknesses rather than to see how physically durable it is in terms of heavy physical abuse.
The BVT (Build verification test) will apply to the hardware side of the project. The BVT is generally a set of fixed tests run on every new build, usually by the team rather than assigned user testers. This is to verify that the build is ready for the users. This information can also be gathered by either the market research agency or via our online event campaigns that fit our fixed criteria or our set type of demographic. If the BVT fails, the build would then be assigned to an appropriate team member and/or developer to fix the issue, and to further improve the condition of the product if it is required or necessary for the build to be even more versatile or easy to use or test for both consumers and testers alike.

How will we know when we’ve succeeded?
We will know when the test has succeeded when the users are able to navigate through the application without experiencing any issues such as crashes and bugs. The tutorial should provide the users with useful information as to how to add new plants and use the probe to identify the plant’s pH, moisture and temperature levels. There are other small tutorials on the diagnose function which provides the users with information as to how to treat specific diseases.

Additionally we will know that we have succeeded when the probe is able to collect and transmit data to the app without any errors. The probe should be able to transmit pH, Moisture and temperature levels to the app without any issues.

In terms of the AI, we will know we have succeeded when it can accurately diagnose any plant disease, identify plant species, and the chatbot is working to solve any user queries and to solve any technical and plant issues. The AI should also be able to identify the plant’s current disease and species with information from the Green Thumb database.
