Plans and Progress

So What Will Green Thumb Actually Do?

Green Thumb comprises two primary pieces of technology: a soil probe and a phone app. These technologies work together to assist plant growers in tracking and maintaining plant health. The probe consists of sensors to measure soil nutrition while the app records these measurements, assesses overall plant health and diagnoses plant disease. Combined, this technology serves as a powerful education tool and database for novice and advanced gardeners alike.

The Green Thumb soil probe is a circuit fitted with sensors to measure the temperature, moisture and pH of a plants’ soil. To collect measurements, the probe is placed directly into the soil of a pot and is triggered by a button on the Green Thumb app to collect measurements. These measurements are transmitted to the app through Wi-Fi and stored in a database. The probe is battery powered and portable so that it can be used to measure soil health in countless pots around the home. The ease of use and portability appeals to a wide market of plant growers while saving them money. The soil probe is the essential tool for this project which gives Green Thumb a competitive advantage against other plant health apps on the market.

The primary function of the Green Thumb app is a plant health database. Upon signing up, users are prompted to build their first plant profile in the app. They are asked to identify the plant species, if they are unsure, users can scan the plant with their camera and using image recognition, the app will automatically identify the plant type. Once the species is identified, the app will be able to provide specific information and recommend the ideal values for soil health variables measured through the probe. When using the probe, the app will harness AI to determine the health of the plant based on results of the probe measurement. If the plant is classified as unhealthy, the app will be able to determine the cause and provide suggestions to restore the plant back to health.

Plant profiles store the data received from the soil probe to allow users to look back over historical data and compare variables through helpful infographics that can be viewed over a week, month or annual timeframe. Additionally, users can also upload photos of their plant to its respective profile to track growth and changes in its appearance. Over time, the Green Thumb AI will continually process the probe data to detect patterns and provide users with further advice. For example if the AI detects some gradual changes in soil health due to changing weather, the chat bot would read: “Your soil conditions were ideal in summer, but now that it’s winter the soil temperature is dropping fast. Try placing your plant in a sunnier position during these colder months to keep up the healthy growth!”

The final innovative function of the Green Thumb app is the powerful diagnosis tool. If a user notices a change in their plant’s appearance and is concerned that their plant has become diseased, they can use this tool to diagnose the disease and learn how to restore their plant. When launching the diagnosis tool, users are instructed to upload photos of the affected area of the plant. These photos are run through Green Thumb’s catalogue of plant disease images to identify possible diagnosis’. Next, the user is required to use the soil probe to take soil readings. The diagnosis tool compares these results to the values set in the plant profile and the images to determine the cause of the disease. For example, if images display white spots on leaves and the soil probe detects high nitrogen levels the diagnosis tool will determine that spider mites are the cause of plant decay. This diagnosis is then stored in the relevant plant profile so users can track their plants journey back to health. Through machine learning Green Thumb’s diagnosis tool will be highly accurate and mitigate the risk of mistreatment.

How Green Thumb Began

Bridget, the creative mind behind Green Thumb, has always loved gardening - often getting in trouble from her partner for bringing home yet another plant! When tasked with coming up with a project plan in Assignment 1 she knew she wanted to use technology to make gardening easier but wasn't sure where to start. Below is the process that led to the conception of Green Thumb.

Then along came ABRAIN...

ABRAIN was formed to tackle Assignment 2. Arvee, Nikhil, Idris, Adrian, Renee and Bridget met (somewhat awkwardly) for the first time and each introduced their project ideas to the group. The group decided that we wanted to pursue Green Thumb as their group project for the next 2 assignments as we felt it appealed to a wide audience, solved a real problem and could empower and educate people. Additionally, we were pleased to discover that Nikhil actually had prior engineering experience building circuits with Arduino so this was a significant motivator for our decision as we were confident we could actually build what the project was suggesting.

As a group, we assessed the Green Thumb project and brainstormed ideas to improve it. Ultimately, that meant removing the social media functions proposed by Bridget in assignment 1. Of all the components to Green Thumb, the group felt that this was the least essential and that it would strain resources moving into assignment 3. With this refined version of the project, we confidently submitted assignment 2 and eagerly awaited feedback

Probe Circuit

The original idea for the probe and its circuit was that we would use an Arduino nano but the team’s lead programmer and developer later found out that using a simple D1 Mini board would turn out to be much simpler as all we needed was for the data to be transmitted to the local network for the app to show the user rather than to store and do anything further to it before sending it via an ESP8266 network module. This drastically reduced the size of the Green Thumb to just a little bigger than a stick of gum. Furthermore, Nikhil also discovered that the DH22 air temperature and humidity sensor/module originally thought to be used could easily be damaged with soil being able to enter its housing either while planting, inserting or watering the plant/pot. So Nikhil found a better solution although it would make the final product a bit bulkier. The D1 Mini board could then be paired with the prototyping and designing app called Blynk. The Blynk app receives the values and converts them to digital readable characters from the analogue value if necessary and the developer, Nikhil has made an arrangement of different widgets which are to be dragged and dropped onto the interface.

The group agreed to this new tech but unfortunately, there were delays with shipping. To avoid not having any artefacts for this probe, we created a schematic so we could visualise how the probe would work.

Database Concept and Cybersecurity Plan

Green Thumb in essence is a database that tracks plant health. Therefore designing and securing the database is an essential step in working toward the completion of this project. As no members of our team have experience programming databases, we determined that we would craft a concept of our databases and the relationships between them. The first logical step was to work out the types of data we would be collecting.

Now that we had identified the types of data that Green Thumb will be collecting and classified our entities and attributes, we had to assign our data types and keys. Names and general information are assigned to the data type of varchar and numerical information is assigned to the data type of integer. As for special information that requires decimal points, such as temperature, the data type of double is used.

Then we defined the relationships between the data in an ER Diagram using Lucidchart. We chose to use Lucidchart as it allowed us to create a diagram of our database without any need for programming. See below.

Although not normally represented within a database ER diagram, we wanted to demonstrate what data the Green Thumb AI would have access to. We felt this was important because the AI needs to have access to all the probe, disease and species data in order to feed the machine learning model and provide users with insights into their long term plant health. Additionally, this data is essential for our chatbot to be able to answer users questions. As such, we created a new frame within the ER diagram to show the type of data that is being accessed and monitored by Green Thumb’s AI.

When it comes to hosting our secure database and also collecting and storing user information, we looked into online hosting servers such as HostGator or GoDaddy. Both are great options, however, for the time being, the option we will proceed with is to host our own server. This would be logical as we will need to save some resources as a small start up. In the future, it may be necessary to consider outsourcing to services such as HostGator or GoDaddy to avoid maintenance costs, downtime issues such as power failure, essentially making a more efficient and stable server. It could also be worthwhile investing in a cloud service and having them manage our servers in the future.

To minimize the risks of data extraction, only relevant information will be stored/accessed on user’s devices and not the entire database. In terms of database software, there are two main options, SQL Server (MS SQL) is a database service created by Microsoft and the other being MySQL, an open-source option owned by Oracle Corporation. At this point in the project, either option will suffice, with the main trade off being flexibility of MySQL’s open-source nature and SQL Servers’ security. Both software will be able to upscale with the project as it gets bigger. Cloud computing is becoming more efficient and useful for organizations, so considering this option would be ideal as the project matures and the need for having this service arises. There are great options for achieving this such as: Microsoft Azure, Amazon Web Services and Oracle’s MySQL Cloud Service (for mySQL). As one of the team (Adrian) has previous, although limited, experience with using Oracle inventory database application in the past, we will opt to continue with mySQL. This can however be easily changed in the future if it is not wanted.

With our finished ER Diagram we decided to address how we would protect our data. It became apparent that some of the information that we will be storing in the databases are private and confidential. This applies to both customer/user information but also the Plant species database that is owned by Green Thumb. After some research, we have come up with some potential strategies we can implement to secure our data, outlined below is our cybersecurity plan

Databases contain lots of sensitive information, as far as the Green Thumb database is concerned some sensitive information will include: User’s shipping addresses, names and email addresses. The database will also have all the information and data relating to plant species and the relevant information pertaining to it. It would be easy for anyone with the appropriate skillset to access this information without the knowledge of Green Thumb and it’s users. The data leak can be damaging to both the organisation and it’s users.

There are a variety of ways we can secure our databases. The potential security we can implement is Masking, Redaction and Encryption. We will also potentially implement a backup of the databases in the case of hardware malfunctions and system crashes.

Masking is a strategy we can use to limit the amount of information that is visibly accessed. Information will appear as normal in the database, however some information will be substituted for “false” data or completely redacted. Similar to masking, redaction is the removal of visible information in a database. If the database cannot be read or if the sensitive information is covered up, it cannot be copied or stolen. Masking can be achieved in Database software such as MySQL.

Encryption converts the database into an unreadable format. If a user does not have the appropriate decryption key, it will not be readable. Encryption can be made possible through various applications, MySQL has some encryption capabilities, it is also possible to use other software such as Windows’ inbuilt encryption software BitLocker to encrypt the server the databases are located in.

A database backup will be useful in the event that the original database is somehow compromised, destroyed or made un-accessible. With a backup you would be able to restore to previous versions of the database or restore the entire database when needed. This is made possible through software and services such as: ShadowProtect and Veeam, it is also possible to manage backups within MySQL. The management of backup systems could potentially be outsourced to other organizations.

Accessibility of the database information can be limited within the organisation. Only authorised people can view certain sensitive information. The less accessible the data is, the less chance of a data breach. This can be done within database applications. In Green Thumb’s situation, MySQL provides appropriate tools to grant or restrict the access to the database to certain authorised users only.

  • A Chatbot to answer plant questions and solve technical queries when using app
  • A Visual recognition system to identify plant species and disease
  • Machine Learning models to classify plant health and disease via decision trees and provide long term health insights and analysis

Chatbot

The chatbot will be built using IBM Watson Assistant. The chatbot is built by creating ‘skills’ which use intents, entities and dialog to have a conversation with customers. An intent is how the bot understands and reads the customer questions.To create an intent, a range of similar questions that require similar answers are input and classified together. Entities hold the information that is used to answer questions and are collected within synonyms and definitions. The dialog is then created to act as a script for the chatbot to converse with customers. The dialog is triggered by intents and entities detected in a customer’s question. The chatbot then stores analytics from these customer interactions so that it can be constantly improved over time.

Machine Learning Model

For the machine learning model we will be using IBM Watson Machine Learning. Here we will enter data curated by horticultural experts to teach the model our product specific information. IBM Watson uses ‘transfer learning’ which enables machine learning to be executed and tailored quickly and easily. The transferred learning is a three layered model made up of:

  1. General knowledge
  2. Industry Specific Knowledge
  3. Customer specific learning

These three layers enable IBM Watson to learn more from smaller sets of data and also ensures that your business data remains your own. The data is shared on a strict one-way path from bottom to top, meaning that any customer specific data is safe from being distributed.
